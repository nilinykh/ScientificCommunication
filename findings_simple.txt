QWEN vs HUMAN — short notes
===========================

what the data is
- human_human = 20 short image captions (~83 words each)
- qwen advisor / journalist / writer = 20 image-grounded stories each (~400 words)
- human domains (for comparison) = 4 sets of 100 docs: arxiv, politics, wsj, wp_stories

what t_ means
- t_word = total words per document
- t_punct = total punctuation marks per document
- t_sent = total sentences per document
- they are PER DOCUMENT means, not corpus totals
- if you want corpus totals, multiply by number of docs

how to read the variety columns
- simp_X_var = unique-X / tokens. inflates on short texts. unreliable across length.
- corr_X_var, root_X_var = length-corrected. use these for fair comparison.

main findings
1. qwen writes way longer than the human stories (400 vs 83 words). this is just
   because the prompt asks for a story. not a style signal.

2. all three qwen roles look basically the same. the role tag barely changes the
   structural features. biggest role gap is only ~10%, while between human
   domains it is hundreds of percent.

3. qwen uses a wide range of nouns and adjectives. corr_noun_var ~4.7
   and corr_adj_var ~3.0. that is higher than the short human stories (1.9 and 1.2)
   and close to, or above, the richest human domain (wp_stories at 4.7 and 3.1).
   the main point is that qwen is lexically rich, maybe a bit too uniformly so.

4. qwen never uses interjections (oh, wow, hey). human stories do (corr_intj_var
   0.61 in wp_stories). this is a clear missing feature.

5. qwen never uses symbols ($, %, §). human news does (corr_sym_var 0.33 in wsj).

6. qwen sprinkles numerals consistently (corr_num_var ~0.43). human stories do
   not. so quantification is a qwen habit.

7. qwen recycles fewer determiners than humans. corr_det_var 0.33 in qwen vs
   0.80 in human_human. qwen reaches for more determiner types (every, each,
   those, neither...). this makes it read slightly more formal.

8. qwen sits in the MIDDLE of the human range for subordinators and adverbs.
   it does not commit to one register. it is neither chatty-story nor terse-news.

9. compared to the 4 human domains:
   - human domains differ from each other on register markers
     (interjections, symbols, subordinators, adverb tails)
   - qwen does not show those register markers at all
   - so the qwen output looks like an average of all human registers, with the
     register-specific signals smoothed out

10. the lexical (word-level) analysis agrees: qwen overuses scene-description
    words (eye, face, hand, room, light, gaze, moment, tension, silent, perhaps)
    across every role. humans use more deictic/temporal words (say, year, have,
    make, time, day).

bottom line
- qwen mostly produces one register: atmospheric scene narrative, around 400 words,
   lexically rich, with weak domain-specific markers.
- changing the system role (writer / journalist / advisor) does not change the
   register much.
- the things qwen is missing, especially interjections, symbols, reporting verbs,
   and more varied document length, are also the features that separate the human
   domains most clearly.
